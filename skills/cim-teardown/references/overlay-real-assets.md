# Overlay: Real Assets / Single-Site

Use for site-driven businesses where unit throughput, asset condition, permits, and local demand drive cash flow. Examples: car wash, self-storage, convenience retail with one core site, marina, service bays, small venue, specialty outdoor asset.

## Decision lens
- Can one site reliably produce the cash flow implied by price?
- Are throughput, uptime, and local demand real or dressed up?
- Is maintenance or deferred capex hiding in current margins?
- Is expansion or add-on growth actually permitted and serviceable?

## Driver tree
Default equation:

`units/visits x revenue per unit - labor per unit - utilities per unit - consumables per unit - site occupancy costs - maintenance capex burden`

Do not default to SaaS metrics like NRR or RevOps ownership unless the asset actually has subscription economics and those metrics are central.

## Mandatory denominators
Always ask for the operating denominators that prove the story:
- volume counts such as cars per day, visits per bay, or occupied units
- mix by service tier or membership type
- membership penetration, churn, and tenure when a membership program exists
- utilities per unit where water, power, or gas meaningfully drive margin
- downtime, uptime, queue length, and throughput constraints
- labor hours per unit and staffing by shift
- capex cadence, maintenance logs, and repair history
- zoning and permit status for any add-on expansion claim

## Typical gating items
- Volume claim does not reconcile to POS, gate counts, or equipment logs.
- Margin looks strong only because maintenance or utilities are understated.
- Membership base has weak tenure or high churn relative to the valuation case.
- Throughput is capped by site layout, staffing, or downtime.
- Expansion thesis depends on permits or zoning that are not yet real.

## Kill criteria examples
- `hard pass`: actual volume or revenue per unit is structurally below the level needed to clear the required yield.
- `price reset`: normalized maintenance capex or utility burden reduces site NOI or EBITDA below underwriting.
- `pause`: permits, zoning, or environmental constraints block the planned expansion or add-on service.
- `pause`: downtime or equipment reliability indicates recent performance is not sustainable.

## Evidence asks
Request the minimum evidence that settles the site economics:
- POS or membership export by day, service type, and ticket
- gate counts or volume counts by day and hour
- utility bills by month with rate schedules
- maintenance logs, work orders, and repair invoices
- labor schedules and payroll summaries by shift
- site P&L with rent, taxes, insurance, and repairs broken out
- permits, zoning correspondence, and inspection history
- equipment age list and vendor maintenance recommendations

For each ask, specify:
- system
- fields
- time window
- format
- acceptance criteria

## Owner mapping
Use industry-native owners:
- operator or GM
- bookkeeper or CPA
- equipment vendor or maintenance contractor
- utility providers
- city or county planning / permitting contacts

## External triangulation when thin
If primary records are missing, triangulate with:
- traffic counts
- county parcel and tax records
- zoning and permit portals
- environmental or water-use records where relevant
- reputable maps/reviews for competition density and hours

## Red flags
- Same-store growth is claimed without volume counts.
- Membership revenue is highlighted without churn or tenure.
- Water, power, chemicals, or labor are not shown per unit.
- Site EBITDA excludes recurring repairs or periodic resurfacing/equipment refresh.
- Growth narrative assumes extra lanes, tunnels, or bays without permit support.
